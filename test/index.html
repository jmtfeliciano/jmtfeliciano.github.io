Chapter 1 R Tutorials and Data from USAspending.gov

1.1 How this tutorial differs from other tutorials

R is built as a statistical and computing language. Unlike most R tutorials, the emphasis of this tutorial is to work extensively with tabular data that are represented in R as a data frame object (more on this later). We will then get you started you started with the dplyr package, one of the core data science packages from Posit (Formerly RStudio Inc).

Moreover, although SBA has video tutorials using R within the Precipio platform (more on this later), the materials do not leverage SBA-specific data sets. At the end of this tutorials, we will provide you R code/script templates that you may use in the future for analyses.

1.2 What is USAspending.gov?

USAspending.gov is the official open data source website of federal spending information.

USAspending.gov provides a trove of information and is the official source of data on federal spending idealized by the Federal Funding Accountability and Transparency Act of 2006 (FFATA), which was later amended and expanded by the 2008 Government Funding Transparency Act and the Digital Accountability and Transparency Act (DATA Act), respectively.

The Department of the Treasury—which runs the site—has taken steps to provide transparent information and foster accountability to the public on government spending. The Small Business Administration (SBA) regularly provides USAspending.gov additional (1) contract data daily and (2) awards data twice a month.

1.4 How to request official training from usaspending.gov

The USAspending.gov team is committed to helping audiences of all types learn more about our website’s tools and data.

If your organization would like to request training, you may click (this link) to submit a request. For more information on training requests directly from the usaspending.gov team, please visit the Training section of https://www.usaspending.gov/about for more information.

According to usaspending.gov, a minimum of three participants is required for training requests.

1.5 Signing up for usaspending.gov mailing list

Data on https://usaspending.gov is constantly changing. For example, new data columns were recently added to highlight funding from IIJA (The Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act).

To receive updates and notes on future usaspending.gov changes, you may sign up to the usaspending.gov mailing list by emailing join-usaspending@lists.fiscal.treasury.gov. After your initial email, an automated email response will ask you to confirm that you’d want to be added to the mailing list.

For any questions or concerns related to any key USAspending changes, you may email USAspending.Help@fiscal.treasury.gov.

Important Note on Negative Numbers

Preview of FY 2023 Loan Summary Data as of May 4th 2023

The underlying transaction data that powers USAspending.gov data may cause aggregate numbers for a given FY to be negative. Obligations from a previous FY could be fully or partially de-obligated in the subsequent FY which introduces data rows with negative obligations. In the image shown above, it shows a negative loan aggregate sum for FY 2023 as only 70,000 new loan awards have been issues thus far in FY2023.

1.7 USASpending: Recipient Profile

Preview of Recipient Profile

If you are interested in looking up obligations by recipient, you may also search by name, or if known, by the entity’s Unique Entity Identifier (UEI) or Legacy DUNS (Data Universal Numbering System) number. You may reach this Recipient Profile search page by clicking Recipients under Profiles.

Please note that some federal agencies obligate awards to intermediaries. Even though the funds are eventually distributed to smaller entities, the awards data attribute the funds to the intermediaries as the recipients who directly applied for the funding.
